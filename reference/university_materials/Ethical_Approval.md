## Introduction

Ethics can be understood as the **moral principles that govern a person's behaviour or the conducting of an activity**.

Everybody involved in research, including students, staff, and affiliated staff, has a duty to ensure that their research is ethically appropriate.

Adhering to good ethical practice has several benefits:

- Protects yourself, the university, and any research subjects or participants.
- Ensures compliance with legal frameworks, since some research can lead to legal issues.
- Improves the quality of research.

Failure to comply with ethical requirements is considered a form of **academic misconduct**.

Potential consequences include:

- Having to redo dissertation work.
- Delayed graduation.
- Negative effects on the overall grade.
- Wider implications depending on the severity, such as harm to participants.

---

## Newcastle University's Ethics Policy

Every research project at Newcastle University must be approved, regardless of the department, faculty, or whether the project is student-led or staff-led.

This includes dissertation projects.

Relevant university ethics policy link:

<https://www.ncl.ac.uk/media/wwwnclacuk/research/files/research-ethics-policyandprocedure.pdf>

Selected quote from the policy:

> Students are responsible for highlighting any actual or potential ethical issues arising from their work and bringing them to the attention of their supervisor and then, if required, the relevant review committee.

Ethical approval must be sought **once a project proposal is agreed with your supervisor**.

You must not proceed with the project before approval. This includes:

- Project design.
- Coding.
- Running experiments.
- Other research activities.

You and your supervisor share responsibility for ethical considerations. It is important to keep your supervisor informed as the project progresses.

Be proactive and communicative. Notify your supervisor of any significant changes in your project.

Example:

> You wish to evaluate your project using a human study, but this was not discussed previously.

## The Approval Process: Your Perspective

Every student must submit the ethics form unless stated otherwise.

The form is completed online and should take approximately **15 minutes**.

You should answer all questions precisely and accurately. If the project is not yet fully defined, answer as best as possible. You must also continuously re-evaluate the ethics of the project as it evolves.

Ethics process link:

<https://www.ncl.ac.uk/research/researchgovernance/ethics/process/>

### Process Flow

```text
1. Project Proposal
        ↓
2. Confirm With Your Supervisor
        ↓
3. Fill in the Ethics Form
        ↓
4. Decision
   ├── Automatic approval / low risk → Proceed with your project
   └── Flagged as high risk → Contact your supervisor
```

## Your Project: The Ethical Process

The ethical approval process will be explained by your project module lead and must be carefully followed.

Most projects are low risk and receive automatic approval when the form is submitted.

However, if your project is flagged as high risk, you should contact your supervisor as soon as possible before proceeding.

As your project evolves, you should regularly consider ethical implications.

If a project significantly deviates from the submitted form, re-approval must be sought.

Example:

> If a project includes a human study, and this was not stated in the original form, the project must be reapproved.

When in doubt, seek help and take a conservative approach. Making false assumptions and proceeding with an ethically flawed project is risky and will not end well.

## Projects Completed Outside the EEA

Projects conducted outside the UK and European Economic Area are often classified as **high risk**.

A project may be considered high risk if the work is conducted in a country with different standards.

This includes:

- Using your home country to recruit participants.
- Collecting personal data outside the UK or EEA.

---

## Human Studies

Any human-based study should be carefully planned with your supervisor involved in the process.

Some high-risk indicators include:

- Using gatekeepers to access participants.
- Discussing sensitive topics.
- Deliberately misleading participants.
- Prolonged or repetitive testing.
- Financial inducements.
- Psychological stress.
- Using participants from vulnerable groups, such as children.

If any of these apply, the project may require **full ethical approval**. Full approval should be avoided where possible because of the time involved.

---

## Collecting Personal Data

If you collect and process data governed by GDPR, a contract, a data use policy, or confidential data, you will likely require full ethical approval.

Collecting and processing personally identifiable information may fall into this category.

Example:

> If you collect data in the UK but process it on a Chinese server, this will require full ethical approval.

Ethics toolkit link:

<https://www.ncl.ac.uk/research/researchgovernance/ethics/ethicstoolkit/toolkitdata/>

## Closing Remarks

The ethical process is designed to protect you, the researcher.

Take a conservative approach. Do not simply hope for the best; consider worst-case scenarios.

Proceeding with an ethically compromised project is against university policy.

Treat ethics as a continual process. If your project changes from what you submitted in the ethics form, it must be resubmitted and reapproved.

Caution is advised when conducting projects involving:

- Any work conducted outside the UK and EEA.
- Studies involving human participants.
- Discussion of sensitive topics.
- Collection and processing of confidential data.
- Reading the terms and conditions of any third-party data sources you plan to use.

If in doubt, reach out for assistance. Contact your supervisor first.

## Recurrent Questions

- **Is the project externally funded?**  
  No.

- **Has ethical approval to cover this proposal already been obtained?**  
  No, unless you are working on an existing project.

- **Will anyone be acting as sponsor under the NHS Research Governance Framework for Health and Social Care?**  
  No.

- **Do you have a Newcastle upon Tyne Hospitals reference?**  
  No.

- **Will someone other than you, the principal investigator, or your supervisor for student projects be responsible for the conduct, management, and design of the research?**  
  Yes for industrial projects. If so, name them.

- **Does the research involve human participants, such as questionnaires, focus groups, observation, surveys, or lab-based studies involving human participants?**  
  It might. Discuss this with your supervisor.

- **Does the research involve viewing, using, or transferring sensitive data or personal data as defined by GDPR, or data governed by statute such as the Official Secrets Act 1989, Terrorism Act 2006, commercial contract, or client confidentiality?**  
  Usually, no. Sensitive data and personal data are specific types of data that do not need to be recorded for most projects.

- **Will the research be conducted outside the UK or European Economic Area, or will it involve international collaborators outside the EEA?**  
  Ideally, no. Discuss this with your supervisor.
